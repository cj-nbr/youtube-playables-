# Chess — The Complete Beginner Guide

Chess is one of the oldest and most popular strategy games in the world, played by
millions on boards, phones, and browsers. If you have never played before, this guide
will walk you from the empty board to your first checkmate using the simple,
approachable version available right here.

## What Is Chess?

Chess is a two-player game played on an 8×8 board of alternating light and dark
squares. Each player begins with 16 pieces: a king, a queen, two rooks, two bishops,
two knights, and eight pawns. One side is White and the other is Black. White always
moves first, and then the players take turns. The goal is to checkmate the opponent's
king — that is, to put it under attack in a way it cannot escape.

## The Board and the Pieces

The board is set up so that each player has a row of pawns in front and their other
pieces behind. The queen stands on her own color (the White queen on a light square,
the Black queen on a dark one), and the king sits beside her. Although memorizing the
setup takes a minute, it quickly becomes second nature after a few games.

Every piece moves differently. Pawns step forward one square, or two squares on their
very first move, and they capture diagonally. Knights hop in an L-shape and can jump
over other pieces. Bishops glide along diagonals, rooks along ranks and files, and the
queen combines both. The king moves just one square in any direction but may never move
into check.

## How a Turn Works

In this version the game is local and pass-and-play, so you and a friend share the same
device. To move, click or tap a piece to select it. The board immediately highlights
every square that piece may legally move to, including any captures. Click a highlighted
square to complete the move, then pass the device to your opponent. Illegal moves are
never offered, which makes the game a gentle way to learn the rules.

## Check, Checkmate, and Stalemate

When a king is under attack by an enemy piece, it is in check, and the player must get
out of it on their turn. A king is checkmated when it is in check and has no legal move,
cannot capture the attacker, and cannot block the threat. Checkmate ends the game, and
the player who delivered it wins. Stalemate is different: it happens when the side to
move has no legal move but is not in check, and it ends the game as a draw.

## Getting Started as a Beginner

The fastest way to improve is to build good habits early. Control the center squares
with a pawn move like e4 or d4, develop your knights and bishops before your queen, and
keep your king protected. Always glance at your opponent's last move to see what it
threatened, and use the highlighted squares to find captures and escapes you might
otherwise miss.

This lightweight edition intentionally keeps the rules simple: castling and en passant
are not included, and pawns do not promote. That leaves the heart of chess intact —
outthinking your opponent and hunting the king — while removing the details that slow
down new players. With a little practice you will be spotting checkmate patterns and
enjoying full, competitive games.
