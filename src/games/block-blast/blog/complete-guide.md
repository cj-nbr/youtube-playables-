# Block Blast — Complete Guide

Block Blast is a satisfying color-clearing puzzle played on a 10×12 grid of colored blocks. There are no timers and no swaps — your only action is to click or tap any block that belongs to a connected group of two or more same-colored blocks. The entire group disappears at once, the board reshapes itself, and your score ticks upward. It sounds simple, but the scoring rule turns it into a genuinely strategic game.

## How Block Blast works

The board starts as a 10-column by 12-row field filled with a small palette of block colors. A "group" is any set of blocks of the same color joined orthogonally — up, down, left, or right — with two or more members. When you click a block in such a group, every block in that group is removed in a single move. That is the whole interaction: find a group, clear it, repeat.

## Understanding the scoring system

Scoring is the heart of the game, and it is what makes Block Blast more than a casual clicker. Each group you clear is worth its size squared: a 2-block group earns 4 points, a 3-block group 9, a 5-block group 25, and a 10-block group a hefty 100. Because the reward grows quadratically rather than linearly, one large clear is worth far more than many small ones. Clearing ten isolated pairs gives you only 40 points, while a single 10-block group gives 100. This single fact — size beats frequency — drives every good strategy.

## Gravity and column collapse

After a group clears, the board does not just sit there with a hole. First, every block above the gap falls straight down to fill the empty space, preserving the order of each column. Then, if a column has become completely empty, it is removed and the columns to its right slide left to close the gap. These two rules — vertical gravity and leftward column collapse — are what create cascades and brand-new groups where none existed before.

## Strategy for high scores

To score well, start by growing groups before you clear them. If leaving a color alone for a turn lets gravity drop a matching block into place, you can turn a 3-block group into a 5-block one and more than double its value. Clear from the bottom whenever possible, because lower blocks vanishing cause the blocks above to fall and frequently land next to same-colored neighbors. Most importantly, avoid stranding singletons: a block whose color has no neighbor can never be cleared, and too many of them end your game early.

## Cascades and combos

The most exciting moments come from cascades. A single clear can cause gravity and collapse to form a completely new group, which you can immediately clear, which may form yet another. Chaining several cascades off one opening move can wipe huge sections of the board. Advanced players deliberately empty entire columns to drag distant same-colored blocks next to each other, manufacturing groups that were impossible a moment earlier.

## What ends the game

Block Blast is not endless in the way some arcade games are. Each board is finite, and play ends when no group of two or more same-colored orthogonally adjacent blocks remains. Whatever singletons are left are permanent, and your final score is the sum of every group you removed. The challenge is to maximize that total before the board stalls out — which means balancing the urge to pop obvious pairs against the patience to build something bigger.

## Tips to get started

If you are new, build three simple habits. First, look for the largest group on the board and clear it rather than the nearest pair. Second, work bottom-up so drops reconnect colors for you. Third, keep colors grouped so you do not isolate single blocks. With those habits, your scores will climb quickly, and from there you can start engineering the cascades that separate casual players from the leaderboard.
