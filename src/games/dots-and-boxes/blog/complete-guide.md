# Dots and Boxes — Complete Guide

Dots and Boxes is a two-player strategy game that hides surprising depth behind
dead-simple rules. On this board you play across a 4×4 grid of dots, which forms a
3×3 arrangement of nine small boxes. The goal is to claim more boxes than your
opponent by drawing the fourth and final side of each one.

## The Rules in a Minute

The board starts as an empty field of dots. Players take turns, and on each turn
you draw a single line segment connecting two adjacent dots — horizontally or
vertically. That is the entire move. What makes the game interesting is the reward
for finishing a box: when your line completes the fourth side of a square, you claim
that box in your color and immediately take another turn.

If your line does **not** complete a box, your turn ends and play passes to your
opponent. The game continues until every box on the board is claimed. Because this
grid has nine boxes — an odd number — there is always a clear winner, never a tie.
Each box is worth one point, so the first player to hold five boxes has won.

## The Bonus Turn Changes Everything

The extra-turn rule is the engine of the whole game. Because completing a box lets
you move again, a single turn can close several boxes in a row when they are lined
up. These runs of connected boxes are called **chains**, and learning to see them is
the key to improving. A chain of four boxes claimed in one turn is worth four points
and can swing an entire match.

The flip side is that whoever is **forced to open a chain** usually gives all of its
boxes to the opponent. Opening a chain means drawing a line that leaves a box with
three sides, inviting your opponent to take it — and then the next, and the next,
right down the chain. So the game quickly becomes a contest to avoid being the one
who opens the long chains.

## Beginner Strategy

The first habit to build is simple: **never draw the third side of a box** unless you
have no choice. A box with three sides is a free gift to your opponent. Early in the
game there are plenty of "safe" lines that leave no box with three sides, so use
them and watch the board carefully before every move.

As safe moves run out, someone must eventually open a chain. Your job is to arrange
the board so that person is your opponent, not you. Counting the number and length of
the chains helps you predict who will be forced to open first. This is called
controlling the parity of the game.

## Advanced Play: The Double-Cross

Expert players win with a move called the **double-cross**. Instead of greedily
taking every box in a chain, you take all but the last two, then draw a line that
hands those two boxes to your opponent. It feels generous, but it forces your
opponent to open the next chain for you — keeping you in control until the biggest
chain falls into your hands.

That trade, sacrificing two boxes now to capture a much larger chain later, is what
separates casual players from strong ones. The most common losing mistakes are greed
(taking every box you can) and miscounting the chains. Play patiently, count
carefully, and use the double-cross, and you will win far more games than you lose.

## History

Dots and Boxes was first described by the French mathematician Édouard Lucas in 1889
under the name *la pipopipette*. More than a century later it remains a beloved
pencil-and-paper classic — quick to learn, endlessly replayable, and rich enough to
be studied in combinatorial game theory.
